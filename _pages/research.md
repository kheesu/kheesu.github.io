Modern foundation models learn rich representations of language, culture, and visual information, yet we still have limited understanding of what these representations encode and how they should be evaluated. My research seeks to bridge this gap through evaluation methodologies, semantic analysis, and multimodal representation learning.

## Why Evaluation Matters

As AI systems are deployed in high-stakes contexts — translation, information retrieval, cross-cultural communication — it becomes critical to know not just *whether* a model performs well on a benchmark, but *what* it has actually learned. Benchmarks can be gamed, saturated, or simply misaligned with the competencies they claim to measure. I am interested in building evaluation frameworks that are theoretically grounded and sensitive to the cultural, linguistic, and semantic dimensions that current leaderboards obscure.

This interest motivated my work on cultural evaluation of large language models, where I contributed to constructing multilingual benchmark datasets that probe culturally situated knowledge rather than surface-level pattern matching.

## Why Semantic Representations Matter

Language is not static. Words change meaning across time, domains, and communities — and the representations that encode them should reflect this. Contextual embeddings from large language models offer a powerful lens for studying semantic change, but the relationship between geometric shifts in representation space and human-interpretable meaning change is not well understood.

I am drawn to this problem because it sits at the intersection of linguistics, cognitive science, and machine learning. Understanding how meaning evolves in learned representations matters both for building better models and for studying language itself as a human phenomenon.

## Why Multimodal Systems Are Interesting

Vision-language models have become remarkably capable, yet the structure of what they learn — particularly on the language side — remains largely opaque. Do image encoders trained jointly with text learn linguistically structured representations? What syntactic or semantic information survives the visual encoding process?

These questions matter because multimodal models are increasingly used in downstream tasks that require genuine language understanding. If a model's visual representations encode spurious correlations rather than semantic structure, that will eventually surface as systematic failures. I want to understand these representations at a mechanistic level so that we can build more robust and interpretable systems.

## Unifying Theme

Across these threads — evaluation, semantic change, and multimodal representations — the common question is: *what do neural representations actually capture about language, culture, and meaning?* I believe that answering this question rigorously requires both careful empirical work and engagement with the linguistic and cognitive science literature that has studied these phenomena long before neural models existed.

My goal is to contribute evaluation tools, analytical frameworks, and empirical findings that make it easier to answer this question — and to use those findings to build AI systems that are more linguistically faithful, culturally aware, and interpretable.
